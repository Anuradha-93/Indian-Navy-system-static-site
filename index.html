About INS Vikramaditya

INS Vikramaditya is a modified Kiev-class aircraft carrier and the flagship of the Indian Navy. The vessel was originally built as Baku and served with the Soviet Navy and Russian Navy before being acquired by India. It underwent extensive modifications and refitting before being commissioned into the Indian Navy in 2013.

Purpose and Role

The primary role of INS Vikramaditya is to enhance the blue-water capabilities of the Indian Navy. As an aircraft carrier, it serves as a floating airbase, capable of deploying a wide variety of aircraft for combat, reconnaissance, and search-and-rescue missions. The carrier enhances India's ability to project power and maintain maritime security in the Indian Ocean region.

Design and Features

  • Length: 283.5 meters
  • Beam: 61 meters
  • Displacement: 45,400 tons
  • Propulsion: 8 boilers, 4 geared steam turbines, 4 shafts, 180,000 shp
  • Speed: 30 knots (56 km/h)

The vessel features a Short Take-Off But Arrested Recovery (STOBAR) configuration, with a 14-degree ski-jump for aircraft takeoff. It can accommodate up to 30 aircraft, including MiG-29K fighters and various helicopters.

Capabilities

INS Vikramaditya is equipped with state-of-the-art sensors and communication systems, allowing it to operate effectively in a network-centric warfare environment. The carrier's air wing includes multi-role fighters, anti-submarine warfare helicopters, and early warning helicopters, providing a comprehensive suite of offensive and defensive capabilities.

Crew and Accommodations

The vessel has a crew of approximately 1,600 personnel, including officers, sailors, and aircrew. Accommodations are designed to provide a comfortable living environment for extended deployments, with modern amenities and recreational facilities.

Significance

INS Vikramaditya plays a pivotal role in India's naval strategy, providing a critical platform for power projection and maritime dominance. Its presence enhances deterrence and ensures the security of India's maritime interests.


History of the INS Vikramaditya

The INS Vikramaditya is a modified Kiev-class aircraft carrier and the flagship of the Indian Navy. It was originally built as the Baku and served with the Soviet Navy and the Russian Navy before being decommissioned in 1996. The carrier was purchased by India in 2004 and underwent extensive modifications and refitting before being commissioned into the Indian Navy in 2013.

Construction and Soviet Service

Originally named Baku, the vessel was constructed at the Black Sea Shipyard in Mykolaiv, Ukraine. It was laid down in 1978, launched in 1982, and commissioned in 1987. During its Soviet service, Baku was deployed primarily in the North Atlantic and the Mediterranean. It was equipped with a variety of anti-ship, anti-aircraft, and anti-submarine weapons, making it a versatile and formidable presence on the seas.

Acquisition and Refitting by India

After its decommissioning by the Russian Navy, the vessel was purchased by India in 2004. The ship was renamed INS Vikramaditya and sent to the Sevmash shipyard in Russia for extensive modifications. The refitting process involved removing all the armament and missile launchers and converting it into a Short Take-Off But Arrested Recovery (STOBAR) carrier. A 14-degree ski-jump was added to assist aircraft in taking off from the shorter deck.

Commissioning into the Indian Navy

The INS Vikramaditya was commissioned into the Indian Navy on 16 November 2013. The commissioning ceremony took place at the Sevmash shipyard in Severodvinsk, Russia. Since then, it has become the flagship of the Indian Navy and plays a critical role in India's naval operations. The carrier can accommodate up to 30 aircraft, including MiG-29K fighters and various helicopters.

Operational History

Since its commissioning, INS Vikramaditya has participated in numerous naval exercises and operations. It has sailed across the Indian Ocean, participating in joint exercises with navies from around the world. The vessel continues to serve as a key asset for the Indian Navy, enhancing its blue-water capabilities and projecting power across the region.
